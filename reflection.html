Reflective Statement

When I started this course I thought writing was basically writing. You figure out what you want to say, you find some evidence, you put sentences together until it's long enough, and you turn it in. I had done that enough times in high school that it felt like a process that worked. What this course did, mostly through the three major projects, was show me how much of that process I was just getting away with rather than actually understanding.

The first project, the investigative essay on U.S. stealth doctrine, was where I started learning what it actually means to build an argument from research rather than just use research to support something I already thought. I went into it with a vague idea about stealth technology being interesting, but I didn't have a real question yet. The annotated bibliography forced me to actually read the sources carefully enough to figure out what they were saying and how they connected to each other. That process, sitting with each source and asking what it contributes and what it pushes back against, is where the actual argument came from. I didn't have a thesis until I understood the sources well enough to see what they were collectively pointing toward. That was a different way of working than I was used to, and it changed how I think about research in general. The question isn't what sources support my argument. The question is what the sources are arguing, and what argument that leads me toward.

The essay itself went through real changes between drafts. The first version was basically a tour of interesting facts about stealth planes. It had no central argument and the sources were just kind of stacked next to each other without any real synthesis. When I look back at it now it's obvious, but at the time I thought I had done the work. The revision made me actually ask what I was arguing for and why it mattered, and the answer, that American military doctrine has to evolve because the stealth monopoly is being eroded from two different directions at once, was something I had to figure out by looking at what all the sources added up to. That's composing, I think. Not just assembling information but actually deciding what it means.

Source integration was also something I had to work on. My first instinct was to either drop a quote into a paragraph and move on, or explain the source so thoroughly that my own point got buried. Getting those two things balanced, where the source is doing work in the argument but I'm still the one making the argument, was harder than I expected and I'm still not sure I always get it right. But I'm a lot more aware of it now than I was before.

Project 2 was where genre became something I actually had to think about instead of just take for granted. When you're writing an academic essay there's basically one genre you're working in and the conventions are more or less handed to you. For the multigenre campaign I had to figure out three completely different sets of conventions and make real decisions about why those genres were the right ones for the argument and the audience I was trying to reach.

The biggest thing I learned from that project is that genre isn't just a format. It's a whole set of assumptions about who is reading, what they already know, what they trust, and how much time they're willing to give you. The Reddit post and the podcast aren't just shorter or more casual versions of the essay. They work completely differently, and writing them required me to think about the audience in a way that the essay didn't demand as explicitly. The defense community I was writing for on Reddit is skeptical of anything that sounds like a press release. They respond to sourced arguments and they'll push back hard if something is sloppy. That shaped every decision I made in that post, from opening as a student to including the RAND counterargument to ending with a question. Those weren't just stylistic choices. They were rhetorical choices driven by what I understood about how that audience reads and evaluates writing.

The podcast was the hardest thing I wrote in this course. I had never really thought about the difference between writing to be read and writing to be heard. Sentences that look fine on a page can sound stiff and weird out loud. The rhythm of how you say something matters in a way that it doesn't in print. I kept writing the podcast script the same way I'd write an essay and it kept not working. Reading it out loud as I wrote helped me start to hear what wasn't landing, but I don't think I fully figured it out. The podcast is still probably my weakest piece. What I took from that experience is that writing for audio is a skill that has to be practiced in audio, not on a page, and that's something I want to keep working on.

Rhetoric is the thing that connected all three projects together. In the essay I was thinking about how to make an argument credible for an academic audience. In the campaign I was thinking about how to reach people who wouldn't read an academic essay and convince them to take a specific position. Both of those are rhetorical problems, but they look completely different in practice. The essay relies on careful sourcing, precise language, and a clear structure that walks the reader through the argument. The Reddit post relies on credibility signals that are specific to that community, like admitting uncertainty, crediting sources in the post body, and showing that I understand the counterarguments. The Twitter posts rely on a completely different kind of rhetoric where you have about three sentences to make someone feel like the thing you're saying matters. None of those are the same skill, but they all come back to the same question: who is this for and what do they need from me to take this seriously.

The biggest shift this course made in how I think about writing is that it's not really about the words. It's about the relationship between what you're trying to do, who you're doing it for, and what form is going to get it across. I knew that in some vague way before, but working through three projects that each demanded something different made it concrete. I can't say I have it figured out. The process reflection for Project 1 is honest about that. But I understand the questions better than I did, and I think that's what I'm supposed to be walking away with.
