No Longer Alone In The Dark: Investigating U.S. Stealth Doctrine

On the night of January 17, 1991, F-117 Nighthawk stealth fighters slipped into Iraqi airspace completely undetected. Baghdad's radar operators, staring at their cutting edge Soviet screens, saw not even a blip. No alerts, warnings of any kind. The first sign that Iraq was under attack came when bombs rained down upon their command centers across the capital. According to John Tirpak's account in Air and Space Forces Magazine, Iraqi air defense personnel described the experience as psychologically devastating: they were being destroyed by an enemy they literally could not see. Desert Storm would see the F-117s fly over 1271 devastating sorties without losing a single aircraft. It wasn't just a tactical success; it was the kind of overwhelming technological advantage that makes an enemy feel terrifyingly helpless.

What looked like science fiction was the result of engineering decisions made twenty years earlier in Nevada. Stealth technology transformed from a cool feature into the foundation of American military strategy. The whole doctrine became built around one core idea: we can hit you before you even know we're there. And for about thirty years, that assumption held. It shaped not just how the U.S. fought wars, but whether potential adversaries dared to provoke confrontations in the first place. When you know the other side can take out your air defenses on night one without you being able to fight back, it changes your calculations pretty quickly.

But something has changed. After researching current defense literature and technical analyses, it becomes clear that the American monopoly is eroding, perhaps from two different directions at once. China has developed stealth fighters specifically designed to counter U.S. tactics, while the cost of maintaining our own stealth fleet has spiraled so high that some analysts question whether we can even afford to fly these planes often enough to keep pilots properly trained. This essay investigates how we got to this point and what research suggests may follow. The central question driving this research is: as rising costs and rival capabilities erode the U.S. stealth advantage, how must American military doctrine evolve to maintain air superiority?

To understand why this monopoly is so hard to maintain, it helps to know just how difficult it was to create in the first place. William Bahret, one of the engineers who actually worked on the original stealth project at the Air Force Avionics Laboratory, published a technical retrospective in IEEE Transactions that reveals the brutal trade-offs involved. The basic physics aren't complicated: radar works by bouncing electromagnetic waves off objects and measuring what comes back. A normal airplane, with its curves and 90-degree angles, reflects radar beautifully, which is exactly the problem. Bahret's team needed to build something that wouldn't reflect radar back to the source, but in the 1970s, computers simply weren't powerful enough to calculate how radar waves would bounce off complex curved surfaces. The only option was to design the aircraft entirely from flat panels, each angled precisely to deflect radar in directions away from the receiver.

The result, the F-117, looked bizarre to say the least. Bahret describes it as basically "a pile of flat panels stuck to a frame"; not exactly the sleek fighter jet you'd imagine (1379). Everything about conventional aircraft design had to be thrown out. Speed? Sacrificed for low radar signature. Agility? Not even a whisper. The F-117 couldn't dogfight to save its life. It was slow, handled poorly, and required constant computer assistance just to stay airborne. But it had one overwhelming advantage: enemy radar couldn't track it. That single capability turned out to be worth all the compromises.

The operational results proved the concept beyond any doubt. Tirpak's history documents how the F-117 performed during its combat debut in Panama and then in Desert Storm. What stands out in the pilot interviews isn't just the lack of American losses, it's the psychological effect on the enemy. Serbian air defense operators in Kosovo reported command centers exploding with no warning whatsoever. They were professionals operating sophisticated equipment, and they felt completely powerless. This wasn't like previous air campaigns where defenders could at least see attackers coming and try to shoot them down. Stealth created a sense that death could arrive at any moment from nowhere, and there was nothing you could do about it. That's a fundamentally different kind of warfare.

At this point a critical inflection point emerges: the RAND Corporation report by Michael Mazarr argues that the biggest impact wasn't even on the battlefield, but rather diplomatic. During the 1990s and 2000s, Russia and China both knew that any serious military confrontation with the United States would start with their air defenses getting dismantled by aircraft they couldn't see or stop. That knowledge changed their behavior. They became much more cautious about challenging American interests because they understood they'd lose any conventional war decisively. Mazarr calls this shift in "strategic stability": Stealth didn't just help America win wars, it helped prevent them from happening (45). The deterrent effect of having this technology was arguably more valuable than actually using it. For two whole generations, American foreign policy operated with this enormous advantage just sitting in the background of every negotiation.

So what changed? The first crack appeared when other countries started building their own stealth aircraft. China's J-20 "Mighty Dragon" is the example that keeps coming up in defense journals, and for good reason. When it first appeared, a lot of American analysts dismissed it as just China trying to copy U.S. technology. But Mark Episkopos, a defense journalist who specializes in military hardware, makes a compelling case that this misses the real threat. The J-20 isn't just a Chinese knockoff of the F-22, but rather a design around a completely different strategy. The key detail: it has much longer range than American stealth fighters and carries PL-15 missiles that can hit targets further away than anything in the U.S. arsenal.

Why does that matter? Because American doctrine depends heavily on support aircraft. The F-35 and F-22 have relatively short combat ranges, so they need aerial refueling tankers to operate far from base. They also rely on AWACS planes, the commanding generals that coordinate the battle. Episkopos points out that Chinese strategists have apparently figured this out. Their plan is not to dogfight American stealth fighters. It's to use their own stealth capability to slip past our fighters and destroy the tankers and AWACS planes that our whole system depends on. Kill the tankers, and suddenly those F-35s are too far from home with not enough fuel to get back. It's the kind of strategy that exploits the weaknesses in American doctrine rather than trying to match its strengths head-on.

The external threat from China would be challenging enough on its own, but there's an internal problem that makes it more complicated: the U.S. is struggling to afford operating the stealth fleet it already has. Dan Grazier, a former Marine Corps officer who now works as a military reform advocate, has published some eye-opening analysis about the F-35 program's actual costs. The purchase price is one thing, but the real killer is maintenance. Stealth coatings degrade and have to be reapplied constantly. The sensors need calibration. Everything is specialized and expensive. Grazier dug into Government Accountability Office data and found that the cost per flight hour has gotten so high that pilots can't train as much as they need to. He calls this the "readiness trap". We've built these incredibly sophisticated aircraft, but they're too expensive to actually fly regularly.

The irony here is apparent: The whole point of technological superiority is to give you an advantage in combat, but if the technology costs so much that your pilots aren't getting enough practice, your theoretical advantage starts to evaporate. A less advanced aircraft flown by a well-trained, experienced pilot might actually be more dangerous than a cutting-edge stealth fighter flown by someone who only gets a fraction of the flight hours they need. And you can't just throw more money at the problem; there's only so much budget to go around, and every dollar spent maintaining F-35s is a dollar not spent on something else the military needs.

So where does this leave American air power? Theodoros Kostis, writing in Joint Force Quarterly (which is published by the National Defense University), offers one framework for thinking about what comes next. His core argument is that trying to achieve perfect invisibility is a losing game now. Radar technology keeps improving, and it's spreading to more countries. Even if the U.S. builds an aircraft twice as stealthy as the F-35, adversaries will eventually develop sensors capable of detecting it. The better approach, according to Kostis, is to stop thinking about stealth as invisibility and start thinking about it as confusion.

This is where the "Mosaic Warfare" concept comes in. Instead of sending a few extremely expensive stealth aircraft deep into enemy territory, you flood the battlespace with large numbers of cheaper, autonomous drones coordinated by those manned aircraft operating at safer distances. The drones themselves might show up on radar, but the enemy is now trying to track and respond to dozens or hundreds of threats simultaneously while also dealing with electronic warfare and deception. Kostis uses a chess analogy: it's like using eight pawns to swarm a king rather than relying on a single queen to do everything (135-36). The enemy has too many decisions to make and not enough time to make them correctly.

The practical implementation depends heavily on artificial intelligence. A human pilot can only manage so many things at once, but AI-assisted systems could theoretically allow one F-35 to coordinate swarms of unmanned vehicles. The drones are cheap enough that losing some is acceptable, and certainly more acceptable than losing a $100 million stealth fighter with a trained pilot inside. Kostis argues this approach addresses both problems identified earlier: against China's strategy of targeting support aircraft, drone swarms provide more combat power without the same logistical vulnerabilities, and financially, the approach shifts costs from expensive manned platforms to cheaper expendable units (138-39). In this model, the F-35 shifts from being a lone wolf penetrator to something more like a quarterback directing the offensive play.

There's a significant counterpoint, though. The same RAND report that described how stealth created strategic stability in the 1990s warns that these new technologies might do the opposite. Mazarr's concern is that when both sides have capabilities for rapid, AI-enabled, high-intensity warfare, the incentive shifts toward striking first. If you know the enemy can launch hundreds of autonomous drones that will overwhelm your defenses, you have a strong motivation to take out their capability before they use it. He calls this a "use it or lose it" dynamic, and it's basically the opposite of stable deterrence (67-68). Instead of both sides being cautious because war would be futile, both sides are nervous because the advantage goes to whoever attacks first. This is a considerably less stable situation than the one stealth originally created.

Looking back at Bahret's description of that ungainly F-117, that pile of flat panels that revolutionized warfare, it's striking how specific the advantages were to that particular moment in history. The U.S. happened to solve the stealth problem first, during a window when computers were just powerful enough to make the calculations work but not powerful enough for anyone else to easily replicate the achievement. That head start created thirty years of dominance. But temporary advantages always eventually disappear in military technology. What's interesting is seeing it happen from two directions: China catching up technologically while American costs spiral financially.

The research here points toward a future where stealth matters differently than it did in 1991. Bahret documents where the advantage came from; Tirpak shows what it looked like in practice; Mazarr explains the diplomatic stability it created; Episkopos and Grazier each describe, from different angles, why that stability is now under pressure; and Kostis offers one possible direction the doctrine might travel next, although he acknowledges the risks Mazarr identifies remain unresolved. The picture that emerges is not one of simple decline or straightforward progress, but of a technology whose meaning is shifting in real time, with significant uncertainty about where it lands. Whether the U.S. military can navigate that shift effectively is a question the research raises more clearly than it answers.
