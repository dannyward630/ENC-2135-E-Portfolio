Artifact A: Annotated Bibliography

What this shows

It reflects the process of building an argument from research rather than finding sources to fit a conclusion already reached.

Topic: The Evolution of U.S. Military Doctrine regarding Stealth Technology

Research Question: To what extent has the U.S. monopoly on stealth technology shaped modern military doctrine, and how must that doctrine evolve now that rival nations are catching up?

Bahret, William F. "The Beginnings of Stealth Technology." IEEE Transactions on Aerospace and Electronic Systems, vol. 29, no. 4, Oct. 1993, pp. 1377-85. IEEE Xplore, https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/259548

This scholarly article, written by William Bahret, a pioneer at the Air Force Avionics Laboratory, serves as a definitive primary history of the engineering behind stealth. Bahret walks through the "Have Blue" project and explains the real engineering problems they faced when trying to make an aircraft invisible to radar. What makes this source valuable is that it doesn't just tell the history, but also gets into the actual physics of how radar works and why early computers weren't powerful enough to model complex curves, which is why the F-117 ended up looking like a pile of flat panels stuck to a frame. Bahret's main point is that stealth wasn't just another improvement in aircraft design, but instead completely changed the game. Engineers had to throw out everything they know about speed and agility to focus entirely on making planes hard to detect.

In this essay, I will use this source to establish the baseline of my argument. To understand how the doctrine is changing, I must first explain how difficult it was to create the monopoly in the first place. Bahret's technical explanations provide the evidence needed to argue that the U.S. advantage was based on a specific moment in engineering history that is now passing. This is a peer-reviewed source published in IEEE Transactions, an engineering journal of the highest caliber, and the author is a primary participant in the events he describes, making this highly authoritative. This source relates closely to Tirpak, as it provides the engineering theory behind the history that Tirpak describes. It also sets up a contrast with Episkopos, who shows how that once-unbeatable technological gap has basically disappeared.

Episkopos, Mark. "China's J-20 Stealth Fighter Has One Big Advantage Over America's F-22 And F-35." The National Interest, 21 Feb. 2020, https://nationalinterest.org/blog/buzz/chinas-j-20-stealth-fighter-has-one-big-advantage-over-americas-f-22-and-f-35-125501

In this article, defense journalist Mark Episkopos argues that American air superiority is no longer guaranteed by comparing China's J-20 "Mighty Dragon" to our F-22 and F-35. Episkopos brings to light a key strategic difference: While the U.S. built short-range stealth fighters that depend on tankers, China designed the J-20 with longer range and the PL-15 missiles that outreach ours. His main point is that China is not planning traditional dogfights, but rather to use stealth to slip past our fighters and destroy the tankers and AWACS plans our whole system relies on.

I will use this source to address the "rival nations" aspect of my research question. It provides specific evidence that the U.S. monopoly on stealth is over, not just because other nations have the technology but also because they are developing strategies specifically to counter and exploit the weaknesses of U.S. doctrine. While The National Interest is a trade publication rather than a peer-reviewed journal, it is a reputable source for defense analysis, and Episkopos is a recognized specialist in military hardware. This source directly responds to Kostis, showing the specific threat driving the need for the U.S. to evolve. It also challenges Tirpak's idea about U.S. '90s dominance by proving those days are over.

Grazier, Dan. "Selective Arithmetic to Hide the F-35's True Costs." Project on Government Oversight (POGO), 21 Oct. 2020, https://www.pogo.org/analyses/selective-arithmetic-to-hide-the-f-35s-true-costs

Dan Grazier, a former Marine Corps officer turned military reformer, takes a hard look at the costs of the F-35 program. He argues that the military and defense contractors use misleading numbers to hide how expensive stealth aircraft really are to maintain. Grazier's main point is that keeping stealth coatings and sensors operational creates a "readiness trap", where the cost per flight hour is so high that pilots can't train as much as they need to. He questions whether a doctrine built on expensive, high-maintenance stealth technology actually makes us safer, especially when it means we can afford fewer planes than if we went with cheaper alternatives.

This source is very important for my essay's discussion on the outcomes and limitations of current doctrine. It allows me to introduce a counter-argument that technological superiority does not always equal strategic superiority if the technology is too expensive to use. POGO is a government watchdog known for being critical of defense spending; while this means the source has a distinct bias against waste, Grazier's reliance on Government Accountability Office (GAO) data lends the article significant credibility regarding the financial side. This connects to Mazarr's points about deterrence: if our fleet costs too much to fly, our ability to intimidate adversaries might be mostly on paper. It also pushes back against Kostis, who wants even more advanced technology like drones and AI, while Grazier's saying we can barely afford what we have right now.

Kostis, Theodoros G. "The Future of Stealth Military Doctrine." Joint Force Quarterly, no. 116, 1st Quarter 2025, pp. 132-40. National Defense University, https://digitalcommons.ndu.edu/joint-force-quarterly/vol116/iss4/19/

This peer-reviewed article from the National Defense University explains how U.S. military strategy needs to change. Kostis argues that the old "pure stealth" approach won't work as radar technology improves. He proposes shifting to "obfuscation" and "Mosaic Warfare," where stealth jets like the F-35 control swarms of cheap drones and use AI to confuse enemy sensors. His point is that future stealth isn't about being invisible, but rather about being everywhere at once and overwhelming the enemy's decision-making.

This is my key source for how doctrine needs to evolve. I am using Kostis to describe what comes next and shift my essay from history to future strategy. Published by the NDU, it represents credible military thinking and links Bahret's technical history with Episkopos' modern threats while offering a solution. Where Grazier worries about costs, Kostis provides an answer by moving toward cheaper drone swarms.

Mazarr, Michael J., et al. Disrupting Deterrence: Examining the Effects of Technologies on Strategic Deterrence in the 21st Century. RAND Corporation, 2022. RAND, https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA595-1.html

This RAND Corporation report examines how emerging technologies like stealth, hypersonics, and AI affect deterrence between major powers. Mazarr and his co-authors argue that stealth technology fundamentally changed "strategic stability" in the late 20th century. The U.S. monopoly on stealth gave us a decisive advantage that forced Russia and China to adjust their foreign policy to avoid direct confrontation. Now that these technologies are spreading, the report warns we're entering a less stable environment where stealth's "first strike" advantage could encourage rapid escalation in conflicts.

I'm using this source to analyze the diplomatic and political impacts of stealth technology, addressing the strategic layer of my research. It proves stealth wasn't just about winning battles but about diplomatic leverage. RAND is a federally funded research center, so this report has strong academic credibility. It connects everything: the geopolitical consequences of Bahret's invention, the strategic context for Episkopos's China warnings, and the dangers of Kostis's high-speed warfare vision.

Tirpak, John A. "History of Stealth: From Out of the Shadows." Air & Space Forces Magazine, 1 Nov. 2019, https://www.airandspaceforces.com/article/history-of-stealth-from-out-of-the-shadows/

John Tirpak's article gives an operational history of stealth aircraft, focusing on the F-117 Nighthawk and B-2 Spirit's combat debut. He details the "shock and awe" during the Gulf War, where F-117s destroyed Iraqi air defenses without taking losses. Through pilot and commander interviews, Tirpak shows how stealth changed the psychology of warfare, letting U.S. forces hit heavily defended targets with near-zero casualties. He traces the evolution from secret Nevada test flights to stealth becoming standard for modern fighters.

I'm using this source for concrete historical examples of the doctrine in action. While Bahret explains the engineering, Tirpak shows the combat application, which is crucial for defining what "stealth doctrine" actually meant during the U.S. monopoly. As an Air & Space Forces Association publication, it has a pro-military bias and celebrates these as American triumphs, but it's factually solid on timelines and operations. This pairs with Bahret to cover the "past" section of my essay and contrasts with Grazier - Tirpak highlights operational invincibility while Grazier exposes the financial nightmare of maintaining these aircraft.

Artifact B: Rhetorical Rationale Outline

What this shows

This outline was built during the planning phase of Project 2, before writing the full rhetorical rationale. It maps out the thesis and the five main areas of analysis, which shows how planning and outlining helped organize the thinking before drafting began.

Rhetorical Rationale Outline

Swarm the Future Campaign

THESIS

Through social media posts, a Reddit long-form post, and a podcast script, my campaign argues that the U.S. military needs to move toward Mosaic Warfare doctrine before the stealth advantage runs out and the cost of maintaining what we have gets too high to keep up with.

PARAGRAPH 1 - Process

  • I started with the research essay, so by the time I got to the campaign I already had a solid argument and knew my sources well. That made planning the campaign a lot easier.
  • I drafted each genre separately because they all felt pretty different from each other and needed different approaches.
  • The Reddit post and social media posts felt more natural to write. The podcast script was harder because I had to think about how it would actually sound out loud.

PARAGRAPH 2 - Genre Choices and Conventions

  • I picked these three genres because they work together as a kind of funnel. Someone might see a tweet and then read the Reddit post, or listen to the podcast and go look up the longer version. Each one stands on its own but they also build on each other.
  • For Twitter I used a thread format with a hook at the start and a call to action at the end, which is pretty standard for that platform.
  • For Reddit I opened by saying I was a student doing research, ended with a question to get people talking, and put a TLDR at the bottom because the post is long and that is just what people expect in those communities.
  • For the podcast I skipped the intro music and went straight into talking, kept sentences short, and ended cleanly. I was trying to sound like the defense podcasts that my target audience already listens to.

PARAGRAPH 3 - Successes and Difficulties

  • I think the Baghdad opening worked really well. It shows up in the podcast and the first Twitter post and it grabs attention quickly without a lot of setup.
  • The TLDR at the end of the Reddit post was a good call because the argument has a lot of moving parts and having a summary makes it easier to follow.
  • Twitter was also tough because I had to cut a lot of the nuance to make things short enough. Some of the posts feel a little oversimplified but I think that is just the nature of the format.

PARAGRAPH 4 - Reaching the Intended Audience

  • My audience is military people, veterans, and defense analysts, basically people who already know enough about this topic to follow a detailed argument.
  • I chose platforms that audience actually uses. Twitter and LinkedIn are where a lot of defense debate happens. The military aviation and geopolitics subreddits have people who take sourced arguments seriously. And podcasts are huge in veteran and military communities.
  • I tried to use sources my audience would respect, like IEEE Transactions and Joint Force Quarterly, because that crowd is not going to be impressed by something that is not well sourced.
  • I also made sure to include the counterargument from the RAND report about instability risks. I felt like leaving that out would make the whole thing feel one-sided, and that kind of audience picks up on that pretty fast.
  • The disclaimer at the start of the Reddit post was intentional. Saying I am a student doing research makes it easier for people to engage without feeling like I am trying to lecture them.

PARAGRAPH 5 - Future Improvements

  • The biggest thing I want to change going forward is thinking about my audience earlier in the process. I did not really lock in who I was writing for until the proposal stage, and I think if I had known from the start I might have looked for different sources or focused on different parts of the argument.
  • I want to get better at writing for audio. The podcast is honestly probably my weakest artifact and I think the only way to actually improve at that is to record myself and listen back, not just read it on the page and assume it sounds okay.
  • I also want to keep working on being more concise. Doing the Twitter posts showed me how much tighter writing can get when you are forced to cut things down, and I want to bring more of that into my longer writing too.
